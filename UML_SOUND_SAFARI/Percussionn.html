PERCUSSION

Mobile and Tablet Users

Playing Sounds:

  • Tap the "PLAY" button corresponding to the instrument you wish to hear.
  • Each instrument has its own "PLAY" button.
  • To stop the sound, tap the "Stop" button next to the currently playing instrument.

Viewing Current Sound:

  • The message displayed at the bottom will update to indicate the currently playing sound or if no sound is playing.

Desktop and Laptop Users

Playing Sounds with Mouse Click:

  • Click on the "PLAY" button next to the instrument to play its sound.
  • Click the "Stop" button next to an instrument to stop the sound.

Playing Sounds with Keyboard Shortcuts:

Press the following keys on your keyboard to play respective sounds:

  • p: Piano
  • t: Timpani
  • x: Xylophone
  • c: Cymbals
  • r: Triangle
  • s: Snare Drum
  • b: Bass Drum
  • a: Tambourine
  • m: Maracas
  • g: Gong
  • h: Chimes
  • n: Castanets
  • e: Celesta

Stopping Sounds:

  • Click the "Stop" button next to the instrument or press any other key other than which is not in the above list.

Viewing Current Sound:

  • The text displayed at the bottom will show the currently playing sound or indicate when no sound is playing.

Piano is a versatile and beloved keyboard instrument which produces sound through percussion (striking the keys), with a rich history dating back centuries. Its origins can be traced to early harpsichords of Europe, and its name is derived from the Italian word "pianoforte," meaning "soft-loud." The piano's complex mechanism allows for a wide dynamic range, from delicate pianissimos to powerful fortissimos, making it capable of expressing a vast array of emotions and musical styles. The piano's keyboard consists of 88 keys, arranged in seven octaves. The left hand plays the bass notes, while the right hand plays the treble notes. The pedals below the keyboard can be used to sustain notes, soften the sound, or add other effects. The piano is a popular instrument for both solo performances and ensemble playing. It is often used to accompany singers and other musicians, and it is a staple in classical, jazz, pop, and rock music. The piano's versatility and expressive capabilities have made it a beloved instrument for musicians and audiences alike.

Timpani is a versatile and powerful percussion instrument consisting of a set of kettledrums, typically made of copper or fiberglass. Each drum is fitted with a calfskin or synthetic head, which can be adjusted to produce different pitches. Timpani are played using sticks or mallets, allowing for a wide range of dynamic and tonal possibilities. The history of timpani dates back to ancient civilizations, where early forms of kettledrums were used in religious ceremonies and military processions. The modern timpani, with their adjustable heads and standardized tuning, emerged in Europe during the Renaissance period. Timpani are commonly used in orchestras, concert bands, and other ensembles to provide a deep, resonant bass line and rhythmic foundation. They are often used to create dramatic effects, accents, and sustained tones. Timpanists must possess excellent technique, coordination, and a keen sense of pitch to play the instrument effectively. The versatility and powerful sound of timpani have made them an essential component of many musical compositions.

Xylophone is a versatile and colorful percussion instrument consisting of wooden bars of varying lengths arranged like a piano keyboard. Each bar is tuned to a specific pitch, and when struck with a mallet, produces a bright, clear sound. Xylophones are typically made of hardwood, such as rosewood, padauk, or cocobolo, and may have resonators attached to enhance the sound. The history of xylophones can be traced back to ancient cultures around the world, including Southeast Asia, Africa, and Europe. Evidence of xylophone-like instruments has been found in archaeological sites dating back centuries. Xylophones are commonly used in orchestras, concert bands, and other ensembles to provide a bright, melodic accompaniment. They are often used to create rhythmic patterns, melodies, and accents. Xylophones are also popular instruments for children's music education, as they are relatively easy to learn and can be used to teach pitch recognition and rhythm. The versatility and bright, cheerful sound of xylophones have made them a beloved instrument for musicians and audiences alike.
